# Chapter 12: the 30-day plan

One file: `30-day-plan.template.md`. Copy it into your project, fill in the blanks, keep it somewhere you'll
see it on Monday.

## The template

```bash
cp 30-day-plan.template.md ~/code/your-project/30-day-plan.md
```

Most of it is a schedule you can follow as-is. The part that matters is the three numbers at the top, and
they are blank on purpose.

## The three numbers

The template ships them as `unknown, you must observe this`, and no tool in this repo will fill them in.

**Skills you have right now.** This one you can count:
`tools/skill-doctor.sh ~/code/your-project/.claude/skills` prints it on the last line.

**Minutes per day correcting Claude.** Nothing can measure this but you, with a timer, for a week. It's the
number the whole plan is judged on and it's the one everybody skips.

**Times per week you paste the same instruction.** Same deal. Count them for three days and multiply.

Write them down before day 1. Read them again on day 30. The book's targets are an 80% cut in the second
number and near zero on the third, and those are goals somebody set, not measurements somebody took. Yours
will be whatever they are.

## What success looks like

Day 30, you open the plan and every week has a real skill count next to it in your own handwriting. Not the
target: what happened.

If correction time hasn't moved by the end of week 2, stop building and audit. More skills won't fix skills
that are too vague to act on, and the chapter is direct about that: pause, read every file, delete anything
that hasn't visibly changed an output.

## A warning about the target

"50+ skills" is the headline and it's the least important number in the chapter. The book's own honest
range, in Chapter 11, is 20 to 35 active skills, with diminishing returns starting around 40 and conflicts
arriving somewhere past 50.

Fifteen sharp skills beat forty dull ones. Aim at the pain points on your list, and when the list is empty,
stop.
